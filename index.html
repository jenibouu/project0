David Fincher, Aaron Sorkin, & Breaking the Five-Man Band


At the risk of being obsequious, yes, this is sort of about Harvard.

After a recent rewatch of David Fincher's 2010 hit, The Social Network, I was struck by a number of realizations:

  • That has got to be one of the snappiest, punchiest intro scenes in existence
  • Fincher's classic style with the muted, cool-neutral colouring is unbeatable
  • Wait, is that Rooney Mara playing Erica Albright???
  • Wow, Aaron Sorkin sure can write a tight script
  • A decade ago the smartphone takeover hadn't truly begun
  • Oh My God, this is a total deconstruction of the Five-Man Band

Now, I'm not going to get into a discussion about which of David Fincher's films is his best work, the complicated real life relationship between screenwriter Aaron Sorkin and Mark Zuckerberg, or even the degree of accuracy vs. sensationalism of the film in question. For my analytical frame and the elements of the film I aim to discuss,The Social Network may as well be a work of pure fiction. What I am interested in is the deconstruction of the five-man-band structure in The Social Network .

The Five-Man-Band is a traditional assembly of a strong team in fiction that can be explained as follows:

In The Social Network , however, we get an inverted chart lacking one crucial role: the hero. It is because of this absence that the team of characters we are introduced to cannot possibly work together successfully.
